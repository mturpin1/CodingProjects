Perpetual Motion


The Origin of Perpetual Motion

The theory of perpetual motion was first conceived in the Middle Ages. Scientists came up with the seemingly-impossible notion that a device, able to produce an infinite amount of energy, could be created. Much research was done, however, with man being no closer to creating a device of such powerful potential. Over the years, as science has advanced, is has, for all practical purposes, eliminated the possibility of such as machine.

Notable Creation Attempts

The first notable attempt at creating a perpetual motion machine came from Robert Fludd in 1618, with his 'water screw,' a device that combined a complicated labyrinth of gears and mechanations, including an Archimedes screw. Accoring to its design, water would travel over a wheel, creating power for the screw, which would then travel up to the top of the contraption, going over the wheel again. Unfortunately, it didn't work, and other attempts by members of the scientific world, such as Robert Boyle(17th Century), Jacob Leupold(1724), and Paul Scheerbart(1910) have failed just as miserably.

Perpetual Motion in the Modern World

The First and Second Laws of Thermodynamics clearly state that such a design would go against all laws of physics that currently exist, and so far, no one has been able to create a true perpetual motion machine. Now, machines such as an incredible device made by Reidar Finsrud, create vast amounts of energy that seem to go on forever. However, none of these machines have indeed lasted forever, and they eventually slow down. In spite of that, these advances in energy production could potentially create a source of long-lasting energy for the future--the possibilities are endless.
